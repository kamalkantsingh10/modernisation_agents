Mainframes are not a niche legacy artifact. They are the hidden backbone of global commerce:

- **70% of global financial transactions** still run on mainframes
- **More than three-quarters (75%+) of the top 100 banks** rely on mainframe systems

- In healthcare: **60%+ of US hospitals** still operate at least one critical application on legacy software without cloud-readiness or modern APIs
- Gartner estimates **up to 75% of hospital IT budgets** are consumed by maintaining legacy systems

### Customer Behavior Patterns

Enterprise customers in the mainframe modernization space exhibit a distinctive set of behaviors shaped by decades of technical debt, regulatory constraints, and institutional inertia:

**Behaviour Drivers:**
- **"If it ain't broke" paralysis** — Mainframes process billions of transactions daily with extraordinary reliability. The fear of disruption outweighs the pain of high operating costs — until it doesn't.
- **Incremental modernization preference** — After a decade of failed "big bang" rewrites, **80% of enterprises shifted their modernization strategies** in 2024–2025 toward phased, incremental approaches (Kyndryl 2025 survey).
- **Risk aversion above all** — Risk is cited as the **primary reason organizations delay modernization**. Regulatory scrutiny from bodies like the FCA, OCC, and APRA means any migration failure is not just a technical problem but a compliance catastrophe.
- **ROI-driven decision-making** — ROI from mainframe modernization has **nearly doubled**: enterprises now expect **288% return** from application modernization on mainframe, **297%** from cloud integration, and **362%** from moving workloads off mainframe (Kyndryl 2024 survey).

_Source: [Kyndryl 2024 State of Mainframe Modernization Survey](https://www.kyndryl.com/content/dam/kyndrylprogram/doc/en/2024/mainframe-modernization-survey.pdf) | [CIO Dive — Mainframe Rewrites Fail](https://www.ciodive.com/news/mainframe-application-modernization-hybrid-cloud/727958/)_

**Interaction Preferences:**
- Preference for established vendors with mainframe credibility (IBM, TCS, Capgemini, Kyndryl)
- Pilot projects and proof-of-concept before committing to full migration
- Strong bias toward hybrid cloud — **92% of respondents favour a hybrid cloud approach** with mainframes retained for core operations

### Customer Challenges and Frustrations — The "Big Eight" Pain Points

#### Pain Point #1: The Technical Debt Timebomb

Technical debt in the mainframe space has reached crisis proportions globally:

- **Global technical debt in 2025**: Equivalent to **61 billion days of repair time** — 45% of the world's code is now considered "fragile"
- **Annual waste per enterprise**: The average global enterprise wastes **over $370 million annually** due to inefficiencies and technical debt rooted in legacy systems
- **IT budget capture**: In financial services, legacy technologies — predominantly COBOL-based platforms — consume **70–75% of annual IT spending**, strangling the ability to deliver new digital services
- **Quantified drag**: Organizations lose around **$130 million through delayed modernization timelines** and over **$50 million through ongoing maintenance and integration work** annually

_Primary Frustration_: The money that should fund competitive innovation is being consumed by the cost of staying still.
_Frequency_: Chronic, structural — this is every budget cycle, every year.

#### Pain Point #3: The Documentation Desert

This is arguably the *most underestimated* barrier to modernisation:

- **67% of mainframe systems have zero documentation** — the code IS the documentation
- Production code has been modified repeatedly over 30–50 years, with documentation that was never written or long since lost
- The original architects and developers have retired or died — there is no living person who fully understands some of these systems
- Average legacy rewrite takes **18–24 months** — most of that time is reverse-engineering what the system actually does, not building the replacement

_Primary Frustration_: You cannot modernise what you cannot understand. And you cannot understand it because no one documented it. And the person who built it retired in 2007.
_Service Pain Point_: Consulting firms (IBM, Accenture, Capgemini) charge millions for *assessment phases alone* — just to map what exists — before any modernisation begins.

#### Pain Point #4: IBM Vendor Lock-In and Pricing Escalation — The "Innovation Tax"

IBM mainframe customers face a structural captivity that has few parallels in enterprise software:

- **Annual IBM price increases**: 6%+ on Z-mainframe software offerings, with some European markets seeing **25–26% increases** on specific products (2024–2025 price rounds)
- **MIPS cost trajectory**: ~$3,285/MIPS in 2015 → projected **~$20,340/MIPS by 2025** (20% annual compounding)
- **MLC (Monthly License Charge) trap**: Monthly charges for hardware and software can only be reduced by changing the hardware or stopping use of the software — neither of which is viable for most organisations
- IBM's licensing model based on CPU core utilisation **actively disincentivises** workload optimisation — the more efficiently you run, the more IBM captures the value
- IBM has faced legal scrutiny: accused of bundling unnecessary "catalog software" into Enterprise License Agreements with banks and insurers, using steep discounts on mainframe hardware to lock in software purchases
- As the mainframe customer base shrinks, IBM raises prices to maintain profitability — a reinforcing spiral

**The "Innovation Tax" as of 2026**: By 2026, organisations report this has reached a breaking point — budgets consumed by IBM licensing leave nothing for competitive capabilities.

_Primary Frustration_: Customers feel trapped. The cost of staying is unsustainable; the cost and risk of leaving feels unsurvivable.
_Trust Barrier_: IBM's opaque, complex pricing structure (MIPS, MSUs, MLC, sub-capacity pricing) is deliberately difficult to understand and benchmark.

#### Pain Point #6: The Behavioral Equivalence Wall

This is the technical pain point that kills projects mid-stream — and the one consultants rarely disclose upfront:

- Mainframe COBOL applications are often tightly coupled to the **non-functional behaviors of the mainframe itself**: batch throughput characteristics, specific I/O handling sequences, JCL orchestration, and strict SLA behaviors
- These characteristics **cannot simply be translated** to a new language — they must be precisely re-implemented and then exhaustively tested
- In regulated industries (banking, insurance), **behavioral equivalence** requires regression testing against "golden datasets" and parallel running of old and new systems simultaneously — sometimes for 12–18 months
- Redeveloping a single complex COBOL application in Java can require **600 person-years** of effort (Heirloom Computing estimate)
- Data migration is systematically underestimated: data loss, corruption, and broken interfaces are among the **highest-impact risks** — and they often only surface post-go-live

_Primary Frustration_: The system works perfectly. You're trying to rebuild a plane while flying it — in full view of financial regulators.

#### Pain Point #7: Regulatory and Compliance Pressure — A Double-Edged Sword

Regulation both *drives* and *impedes* mainframe modernisation simultaneously:

**Regulation as a driver:**
- Open banking mandates (PSD2 in Europe, CDR in Australia) require mainframe data exposed via APIs — forcing modernisation of interfaces even if core systems stay put
- DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act, EU effective Jan 2025) requires detailed documentation of IT dependencies — exposing gaps in mainframe system knowledge
- Basel IV capital requirements demand more sophisticated real-time risk calculations than legacy batch-processing mainframes can deliver efficiently

**Regulation as a barrier:**
- Any migration must be approved by and reported to regulators (FCA, OCC, APRA, ECB) — adding 12–18 months to any project timeline
- Regulators do not reward speed — they reward stability. The pace of modernisation is effectively *set* by regulatory approval cycles.
- A failed migration triggers immediate regulatory action: TSB Bank was fined £48M ($62M) by the FCA, with mandatory remediation requirements lasting years

#### Pain Point #8: The Competitive Velocity Gap — The Fintech Threat

The strategic urgency behind all other pain points:

- **Neobanks and fintechs** (Monzo, Revolut, Starling, Chime, N26) launched with zero mainframe debt and cloud-native architectures
- They can release new features in **days or weeks** — incumbent banks on mainframes take **months or quarters**
- A bank's modernisation approach that **preserved $200M in existing mainframe investments** while enabling new digital features showed: time-to-market for new digital banking features decreased by **60%** post-modernisation
- **68% of financial services respondents** expect growth in their mainframe workload (MIPS) — meaning the problem is getting larger, not smaller
- **49% of financial services firms** are actively pursuing application modernisation — updating core systems to modern languages (Java, Go, Python) to close the velocity gap

_Primary Frustration_: The mainframe was built for the transaction economy. The digital economy requires capabilities the mainframe constrains — real-time personalisation, AI inference at the edge, event-driven architecture.

### Horror Stories: Failed & Stalled Modernisation — Case Studies

#### Case Study 1: TSB Bank — The £330M Catastrophe (UK, 2018)

The defining mainframe migration disaster of the modern era:

- **What happened**: TSB attempted to migrate 5.2 million customer accounts from the legacy Lloyds Banking Group mainframe platform to a new Sabadell-built platform
- **Data migration**: Technically succeeded — data moved correctly
- **Platform failure**: The new platform immediately buckled under production load. 1.9 million customers locked out for days to weeks
- **Duration of crisis**: System instability persisted for months (April–September 2018)
- **Customer impact**: 5.2 million customers affected; 225,492 formal complaints in the first year
- **Fraud losses**: TSB suffered **£49.1 million in fraud losses** while systems were compromised — criminals exploited the chaos
- **Regulatory fine**: FCA fined TSB **£48.65M ($62M) in December 2022** — four years after the event
- **Total cost**: Migration cost **£330M** — vastly over budget; total costs including fraud, compensation, and remediation estimated in excess of **£500M**
- **Root cause findings**: Inadequate testing, over-optimistic timeline, failure to run parallel systems long enough, regulatory approvals obtained for plan that wasn't adequately de-risked

## Section 4: Customer Decision Processes and Journey

### Customer Decision-Making Processes

Enterprise mainframe modernisation decisions are among the most complex, high-stakes procurement decisions in enterprise IT. They are:

- **Multi-year in duration** — from initial trigger event to signed contract: 12–24 months is typical
- **Multi-stakeholder** — involving CIO, CTO, CFO, Board, regulators, and operational teams simultaneously
- **Consensus-driven** — no single executive can authorise a £50–500M programme unilaterally; board-level sign-off is standard
- **Risk-anchored** — every stage of the process is primarily about risk assessment, not value capture

**Decision Stages:**

| Stage | Duration | Key Activity | Key Obstacle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trigger Recognition | Ongoing | IBM price increase; talent departure; regulator letter; board pressure | Denial / "good enough" inertia |
| Internal Assessment | 6–12 months | TCO analysis; skills audit; scope estimation | Underestimation of scope; lack of documentation |
| Business Case | 3–6 months | ROI modelling; risk assessment; vendor landscape scan | Fear of committing to a number; 200% overrun history |
| Vendor Evaluation | 3–6 months | RFP; reference checks; proof of concept | Vendor lock-in concerns; trust deficit from past failures |
| Pilot/POC | 3–12 months | Bounded proof of concept on non-critical system | Technical complexity reveals in POC |
| Programme Approval | 1–3 months | Board/executive sign-off; regulatory pre-notification | Budget competition; leadership churn |
| Execution | 12–36+ months | Phased delivery with parallel running | Scope creep; skills shortage; behavioural equivalence testing |

**Decision Complexity**: Rated **maximum** — these are tier-1 enterprise decisions alongside ERP replacements and core banking platform changes.

## Section 6: Strategic Synthesis and Recommendations

### Executive Summary

The mainframe modernisation market represents one of the most compelling and durable opportunities in enterprise technology services. The evidence is unambiguous:

**The Scale is Enormous**: A $7–9B market in 2025, growing to $13–18B by 2030–2033 at 9–17% CAGR. The financial services sector alone commands 32% of modernisation spend, and **70% of all global financial transactions** still run on mainframe technology that is, in many cases, decades old and undocumented.

**The Pain is Acute and Structural**: Companies are not struggling with mainframe modernisation because they lack the will to change. They are struggling because the problem is genuinely hard:
- 70–75% of IT budgets consumed by maintenance, leaving nothing for innovation
- A demographic disaster: average COBOL programmer age ~60, with no pipeline of replacements
- A documentation desert: 67% of systems have zero documentation
- A vendor trap: IBM pricing increases of 6–26% annually, with costs that can only go up
- A terrifying failure rate: 74% of projects fail to complete; 9 in 10 rewrites fail first attempt

**The Window is Closing**: The skills cliff is not a future risk — it is arriving now. The Dutch financial system COBOL crisis is a national-level regulatory concern. The Anthropic Claude Code event of February 2026 — which wiped $31B from IBM's market cap in one day — is the clearest possible market signal that pent-up demand for an escape route is enormous.

### Strategic Recommendations

#### For Enterprises Considering Modernisation

**Recommendation 1: Start with AI-Powered Discovery, Not Big Bang Planning**
Use AI tools (AWS Transform, IBM watsonX Code Assistant, Claude Code) to generate system documentation and dependency maps before committing to a modernisation strategy. This reduces the assessment phase from 6–18 months to weeks and costs far less than traditional consulting assessments.

**Recommendation 2: Adopt an Incremental Hybrid Strategy**
The evidence is clear: big bang approaches have a ~74% failure rate. The winning model is hybrid — retain the mainframe for highest-criticality, highest-throughput workloads (where it genuinely excels), and incrementally modernise edge applications, interfaces, and batch processes using modern languages and cloud-native patterns. **92% of successful organisations use a hybrid approach.**

**Recommendation 3: Address the Skills Crisis Before It Becomes an Emergency**
- Identify all individuals whose retirement would create single points of failure
- Implement structured knowledge capture programmes (AI-assisted documentation)
- Partner with COBOL-to-Java training programmes (IBM Academic Initiative, Kyndryl early professionals)
- Begin vendor selection for skills augmentation services now — do not wait for the crisis

**Recommendation 4: Renegotiate IBM Contracts with Leverage**
The Anthropic Claude Code event has shifted the negotiating dynamic. For the first time in decades, IBM faces credible competition in COBOL modernisation. Use this moment to extract better pricing terms, more transparent MIPS/MSU reporting, and sub-capacity pricing concessions.

**Recommendation 5: Build the Regulatory Case First**
Any modernisation programme in a regulated financial institution must begin with regulators, not technology. Pre-notify FCA/OCC/APRA of modernisation intent early. Build the compliance evidence pack (risk register, parallel running plan, rollback architecture, testing framework) before a line of code is moved. This is what TSB failed to do adequately.

---

#### For Vendors / Solution Providers Targeting This Market

**Recommendation 1: Own the Assessment Phase with AI**
The biggest barrier to modernisation is the documentation desert. Any vendor who can credibly reduce the assessment phase from 12–18 months to 4–8 weeks using AI tooling has a decisive competitive advantage. This is the beachhead.

**Recommendation 2: Specialise in Financial Services Regulatory Navigation**
The FCA/OCC/APRA approval pathway is the longest lead-time item in any bank modernisation programme. Vendors who can demonstrate a repeatable, proven regulatory engagement framework will command premium pricing and close deals faster.

**Recommendation 3: Offer Outcome-Based Pricing**
The market is saturated with time-and-materials vendors. The enterprise buyer is desperate for someone to share the risk. An outcome-based model (pay-on-MIPS-reduction, pay-on-successful-migration) would be a powerful differentiator — but requires genuine confidence in delivery capability.

**Recommendation 4: Target the Mid-Market Gap**
75% of spend flows to large enterprises — but mid-tier banks and insurers face exactly the same problems with far less internal capability and far fewer vendor options. A standardised, productised modernisation offering for the $500M–$5B revenue bank or insurer is an underserved and growing segment.

**Recommendation 5: Position Around the Skills Crisis, Not the Technology**
Enterprise buyers know their mainframes are a problem. What they fear most is the talent cliff. Vendors who lead with "we bring the COBOL expertise you're losing" will resonate more powerfully than vendors who lead with "we have the best migration tooling."

1. **The market is enormous and growing**: $7–9B in 2025, to $13–18B by 2030–2033. Financial services commands 32% of spend. 70% of global financial transactions still run on mainframes.

2. **The pain is real, structural, and acute**: 70–75% of IT budgets consumed by maintenance. 74% of modernisation projects fail to complete. Average COBOL programmer age ~60. IBM pricing increasing 6–26% annually.

The talent cliff is arriving. DORA compliance is forcing documentation. The conditions for a market acceleration are all present simultaneously.

## Customer Behaviour and Segments

### Customer Behaviour Patterns

Enterprise organisations with COBOL estates are shifting decisively toward **incremental, AI-assisted modernisation** rather than high-risk "big bang" rewrites. The prevailing pattern: understand the system first, expose APIs where it makes sense, then gradually refactor or rehost based on business priorities. Organisations are using AI primarily for code comprehension and dependency mapping before committing to any transformation path.

Notably, enterprises given the choice to migrate off mainframes are frequently choosing to **stay on the platform** — using AI tooling to make COBOL more manageable and interoperable rather than replacing it entirely. This "COBOL + cloud" hybrid is the dominant emerging pattern.

_Behaviour Drivers: Skill shortages (retiring COBOL developers), cost of legacy consultants, regulatory compliance requirements, cloud/AI integration demands_
_Interaction Preferences: Pilot projects on a single program before committing to full pipeline; strong preference for human-in-the-loop over full automation_
_Decision Habits: Multi-stakeholder — CIO/CTO set direction, enterprise architects validate feasibility, development teams confirm tooling fit_

### Psychographic Profiles

Enterprise decision-makers in this space share a distinct psychological profile:

**The Paralysed Pragmatist (most common)**
- Knows modernisation is necessary; unsure where to start
- Primary fear: breaking something that handles billions in daily transactions
- Needs: clear starting point, risk-bounded approach, proof of concept before commitment
- Quote-equivalent: *"Slowed not by lack of vision but by a lack of confidence"* (Kyndryl 2025 Survey)

**The Compliance-First Conservative**
- Driven by regulatory audit cycles and risk committee approval
- Will not approve tooling without demonstrable auditability
- Needs: clear documentation of what the AI did, human sign-off gates, rollback paths

**The Modernisation Champion**
- Internal advocate (often an Enterprise Architect or progressive CTO)
- Has budget and mandate; needs tooling that shows fast wins to maintain political support
- Needs: visible progress metrics, clean output artefacts, GitLab/JIRA integration for stakeholder reporting

## Customer Pain Points and Needs

### Customer Challenges and Frustrations

**The talent cliff is the most acute pain point in the market:**
- 60% of organisations using COBOL report finding skilled developers is their single biggest challenge
- The average COBOL programmer is now **55 years old**; 10% of the workforce retires annually
- Institutional knowledge leaves with the developer — documentation is consistently spotty or absent
- 46% of IT professionals report an active, visible shortage of COBOL skills in their teams

**System opacity compounds the talent problem:**
- Monolithic, tightly-coupled architecture makes debugging and testing extremely difficult
- COBOL codebases are verbose and often have outdated or missing internal documentation
- REDEFINES, COPY books, and deeply nested PERFORM hierarchies make automated parsing non-trivial
- Without understanding the system, teams cannot safely change it — they are frozen

**Financial bleeding from maintenance:**
- Legacy system maintenance consumes up to **80% of IT budgets** in some regulated sectors
- Every year spent maintaining rather than modernising widens the competitive gap
- IBM mainframe licensing costs (MLC) are a growing operational burden with limited negotiating leverage

**Business disruption risk is the silent blocker:**
- COBOL systems handle $3T of daily commerce — downtime is catastrophically costly
- The fear of breaking something that "just works" paralyses decision-making
- Teams cannot test their understanding of the system without risking production

_Primary Frustrations: Talent scarcity, knowledge opacity, maintenance cost spiral, inability to prove safety of changes_
_Frequency: Near-universal across all enterprise segments with mainframe estates_
_Sources: [AWS ML Blog](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/learnings-from-cobol-modernization-in-the-real-world/) | [Pragmatic Coders Legacy Stats 2025](https://www.pragmaticcoders.com/resources/legacy-code-stats) | [Optisol - 5 COBOL-to-Java Challenges](https://www.optisolbusiness.com/insight/5-key-challenges-enterprises-face-during-cobol-to-java-modernization)_

### Unmet Customer Needs

**Critical gap #1 — Behavioural equivalence validation:**
The most significant unmet need in the entire market. No current tool can *prove* that migrated code behaves identically to the original COBOL. This is the compliance blocker for regulated industries. As one source puts it: *"the first platform to demonstrate provable behavioral equivalence with referenceable production deployment changes the compliance conversation."*

**Critical gap #2 — Understanding over translation:**
Enterprises don't need code translated — they need their system *understood*. Current AI tools jump too quickly to code generation. The real value is: "What does this program actually do? What business rules live inside it? What will break if we change it?" One vendor explicitly calls this out: *"Understanding your COBOL applications is more valuable than replacing them."*

**Critical gap #3 — Business rule extraction:**
Business rules buried in 30-year-old COBOL are not documented anywhere else. When these programs are rewritten without extracting the rules, organisations *rediscover* them through production failures and compliance violations. No tool does this reliably at scale.

**Critical gap #4 — Full programme scope (not just code translation):**
AI tools today accelerate code analysis and transformation but leave enterprises to handle: data layer migration, middleware replacement, integration re-engineering, test data capture, observability setup, and organisational change management. Customers feel abandoned halfway through.

**Critical gap #5 — Audit trail and regulatory artefacts:**
94% of organisations say regulatory compliance shapes their modernisation plans. Yet current tools produce output that regulators cannot review — no structured, human-readable record of *what the AI found, what it decided, and why*.

_Solution Gaps: Behavioural equivalence tooling; business rule extraction with auditability; structured spec layer as regulatory artefact_

### Barriers to Adoption

**Technical barriers:**
- LLMs have limited token windows — large COBOL programs exceed context limits, causing loss of critical context mid-analysis
- LLMs reproduce text patterns; they do not perform real system-level reasoning — unsuitable for engineering-grade precision without additional scaffolding
- Middleware-specific knowledge (proprietary macros, CICS, DB2 variants) is not in LLM training data — tools fail on organisation-specific extensions

**Trust barriers:**
- 70% of modernisation projects historically failed — enterprises are carrying scar tissue from previous attempts
- AI tools are new to this domain; no enterprise-grade reference customers with verifiable production deployments
- Regulated organisations need to explain AI decisions to auditors — black-box outputs are unusable

**Organisational barriers:**
- 74% of organisations rely on third-party SIs for modernisation — tool adoption must come through or alongside SI partners
- Multi-stakeholder decision cycles (CIO + Enterprise Architect + Compliance + Finance) lengthen procurement
- Internal politics: mainframe teams often resist modernisation that threatens their domain

**Cost/complexity barriers:**
- Full modernisation programmes require business scoping, technical assessment, data migration, behavioural validation, testing, and change management simultaneously
- No tool addresses all of this — customers must stitch together multiple vendors and consultants
- Pilot project cost and effort often exceeds initial expectations, stalling momentum

### Service and Support Pain Points

- **SI dependency:** 74% outsource to third parties — meaning the tool is often one step removed from the organisation; SI quality determines project outcome, not just the tool
- **Documentation gaps persist post-analysis:** Even after AI analysis, organisations find documentation is incomplete for edge cases only discovered during migration
- **Test data is consistently overlooked:** Teams reach code conversion stage only to stall because no one planned for capturing real production test scenarios

**Expectation vs Reality:**
- Enterprises expect AI to "understand" COBOL the way an experienced developer would — it doesn't; it pattern-matches
- Vendors promise "modernise in months" — enterprises find the *analysis* takes months, the *migration* takes years
- "AI-assisted" often means "AI suggests, human validates everything" — the human effort is still enormous

**Value perception:**

- The spec layer gap (structured intermediate representation between analysis and implementation) is widely felt but rarely named

## Customer Decision Processes and Journey

### Customer Decision-Making Processes

Enterprise COBOL modernisation decisions are **long-cycle, multi-stakeholder, risk-weighted** B2B decisions. They are not impulse buys — they are organisational commitments that require internal alignment, external validation, and political cover.

**Typical decision timeline:** 6–18 months from pain point recognition to contract signature; longer in government and highly regulated financial services.

**Decision complexity:** Very high. Must satisfy technical, compliance, financial, and organisational dimensions simultaneously. A single objection from any stakeholder (compliance, finance, mainframe team) can stall or kill the decision.

**Key stages:**
1. **Pain catalyst** — A triggering event (key COBOL developer resigns, audit finding, IBM MLC price increase, board-level transformation mandate)
2. **Internal scoping** — CIO/CTO defines the problem; Enterprise Architect begins landscape assessment
3. **Market scan** — Analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester), peer conversations, vendor briefings
4. **Shortlist** — 2–4 vendors/tools identified; often shaped by existing SI relationships
5. **Proof of Concept** — Structured pilot on a representative COBOL program (typically 4–12 weeks)
6. **Business case** — ROI model built from PoC results; presented to CFO/board
7. **Procurement** — Vendor approval, legal review, security assessment (3–12 months in regulated sectors)
8. **Phased rollout** — Never "all programs at once"; complexity-ordered wave approach

_Decision Timelines: 6–18 months (commercial banking); 12–36 months (government); faster for internal tooling vs external procurement_
_Sources: [Xenoss CIO Guide](https://xenoss.io/blog/cobol-modernization-cio-guide) | [IN-COM Mainframe Firms 2025](https://www.in-com.com/blog/top-mainframe-modernization-firms-in-2025-2026-the-leading-vendors-powering-digital-transformation/) | [ModLogix Business Case 2025](https://modlogix.com/blog/how-to-build-a-business-case-for-application-modernization-in-2025/)_

### Decision Factors and Criteria

**Primary criteria (must-haves):**
1. **Demonstrable accuracy** — Does the analysis output correctly identify what the COBOL program does? Errors here are disqualifying
2. **Industry-specific experience** — Has the vendor/tool worked with banking/insurance COBOL specifically? Sector-specific compliance, CICS, DB2 patterns matter
3. **Clear methodology** — Is there a structured, repeatable process? "We run AI on it" is not reassuring to enterprise buyers
4. **Security and data handling** — COBOL source code is extremely sensitive IP; data residency and model training policies are scrutinised
5. **Human-in-the-loop design** — Full automation is viewed with deep suspicion for production-critical systems

**Secondary criteria (differentiators):**
- Speed of analysis (time to first insight on a pilot program)
- Quality and readability of output artefacts (business stakeholder-friendly)
- Integration with existing delivery tooling (GitLab, JIRA, ServiceNow)
- Vendor/tool track record and reference customers
- Total cost of ownership (licensing, consultancy, internal effort required)

**ROI benchmarks enterprises expect to see:**
- 288–362% ROI from modernisation programmes (Kyndryl research)
- 40–60% reduction in total cost of ownership (mainframe → cloud-native)
- Delivery of new products in weeks rather than months

## Strategic Synthesis and Recommendations

### Market Verdict

**The timing is exceptional.** The AI-assisted COBOL modernisation market is at an inflection point:
- The problem is urgent (skill cliff, cost pressure, compliance deadlines)
- The technology (LLMs + agentic pipelines) has just become capable enough
- The market signal (IBM -13% on Anthropic COBOL announcement) shows enterprise attention is focused
- The open-source space has one reference project (Microsoft/Bankdata) but no dominant leader
- The most critical unmet needs (business rule extraction, spec layer, middleware knowledge, delivery integration) remain unaddressed by any competitor

This is the window. It will not stay open indefinitely — IBM, AWS, and new VC-backed entrants will close gaps within 12–24 months.

---

### Strategic Recommendations

#### Recommendation 1 — Lead with "understand first, convert later" as the brand position

Every competitor either skips straight to code generation or gets lost in platform-level complexity. Your project's core principle — *no code generation until the full program is understood* — is not just a technical choice, it is the market's most wanted behaviour. Make it the headline. This is what enterprises have been burned by not having.

**Implication:** The README, documentation, and any public-facing description should open with this principle, not with the technology stack.

---

#### Recommendation 2 — Name the spec layer explicitly and prominently

The structured intermediate representation (SQLite spec tables) is your most architecturally novel contribution. The market doesn't have a name for it yet — which means you can name it. "The spec layer" or "the migration specification" positions it as the missing link between analysis and implementation that the entire industry has been working around.

**Implication:** Lead architecture documentation with the spec layer design. Make the SQLite schema public and well-documented from day one — it is the artefact that makes the whole pipeline composable.

---

#### Recommendation 3 — Position the delta-macros-mcp as a pattern, not just a component

Every large COBOL shop has proprietary middleware. The delta-macros-mcp is not just a component for Delta macros — it is the *pattern* for how any organisation can inject their middleware knowledge into the pipeline without modifying the agents. This generalises.

**Implication:** Document the MCP server as an extension pattern. Name it something like "Knowledge MCP" or "Middleware Context Server." This becomes a selling point for any organisation with proprietary macros, transaction monitors, or custom COBOL runtime extensions.

---

#### Recommendation 4 — Design the pilot experience deliberately

Enterprises evaluate 2–3 tools in parallel on the same COBOL program. Structured pilots convert at 68% vs 23% for unstructured ones. Your first-impression output — what Viper Phase 1 produces from a single COBOL file in the first minutes — is the make-or-break moment.

**Implication:** Invest heavily in the quality and clarity of the Viper Phase 1 output. The paragraph call graph (Mermaid diagram), complexity score with reasons, and data dictionary need to be immediately impressive to an Enterprise Architect who has seen many tools before. "Fast first insight" is a purchase criterion.

---

#### Recommendation 5 — Design for SI delivery from the start

74% of enterprises rely on systems integrators for modernisation. SIs evaluate tooling by asking: "Can I build a repeatable delivery methodology on top of this?" The BMAD pipeline + GitLab integration is your answer — but it needs to be framed as SI-deliverable, not just dev-tool-accessible.

**Implication:** Write a "delivery methodology" section in the documentation that shows how an SI would run a client engagement using the pipeline. This makes the project feel like a programme delivery framework, not just a code analysis tool — which is exactly what the market is missing.

---

#### Recommendation 6 — Publish a worked example with a public-domain COBOL program

Both Anthropic and AWS claim "quarters not years." A structured open-source pipeline that *demonstrates* this claim with a real, public-domain COBOL program (there are several well-known examples: the COBOL-85 sample programs, the DoD open-source repositories) is highly credible. It becomes the reference pilot that enterprise evaluators run against their own programs.

**Implication:** Prioritise a clean end-to-end worked example as part of the initial release: one COBOL program → Viper output → Crane output → Shifu business markdown + spec layer → Oogway architecture recommendation. This is your proof-of-concept story.

---

#### Recommendation 7 — Tai Lung as the behavioural equivalence answer

The single biggest unmet need with no current solution is behavioural equivalence validation — proving migrated code behaves identically to the original COBOL. This is the compliance blocker for regulated industries. Tai Lung (QA agent) is your answer, but it needs to be positioned as such.

**Implication:** When defining Tai Lung's scope, explicitly include behavioural equivalence testing as a design goal. Even a partial answer (regression test generation from the spec layer's business rules) positions your pipeline as the only one working on this problem in open source.
